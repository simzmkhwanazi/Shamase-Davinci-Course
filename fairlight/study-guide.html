Module 01

Introduction to Audio & the Fairlight Page

Chapter overview

Fairlight is DaVinci Resolve's built-in digital audio workstation (DAW). This module orients you in the page, the meters and the core vocabulary so every later skill has a home. Because Fairlight and the Edit page share one timeline, you move into serious audio work without exporting or re-importing anything.

Detailed lesson notes

The page layout

  • The timeline sits in the centre with one lane (track) per audio element.
  • The Index (left) lists tracks and markers and controls track visibility.
  • The Mixer (right) is the channel-strip view: a fader, pan, EQ, dynamics and bus routing for every track, plus the buses and the Main.
  • Transport controls and the toolbar run along the bottom.

Reading levels

  • Digital meters read dBFS (decibels relative to full scale). 0 dBFS is the ceiling — go above it and you clip (distort).
  • Meters turning red at the top warn you of clipping. Pull the level down to restore headroom.
  • Perceived loudness for delivery is measured separately in LUFS (covered in Module 7).

Core vocabulary

  • Clip: one segment of audio sitting on a track. Track: the lane that holds clips. Bus: a destination that sums multiple tracks.
  • Playhead: your current position in the timeline.
  • Clip gain changes one clip's level; the track fader changes the whole track's level. Set clip gain first, then mix with faders.
  • Track formats describe channel count: mono (1), stereo (2), 5.1 (6), adaptive (variable).

Transport & navigation

  • Spacebar plays/stops. J-K-L shuttles: J back, K stop, L forward — tap L or J repeatedly to speed up.
  • Zoom in horizontally to see waveform detail down to sample level; this precision underpins editing and repair.

Key takeaways

  • Fairlight is a full DAW inside Resolve, sharing the Edit page's timeline.
  • 0 dBFS is the hard digital ceiling — never clip; keep headroom.
  • Clip gain = one clip; fader = whole track. Balance clip gain before mixing.
  • Solo (S) and Mute (M) on track headers are non-destructive monitoring tools.

Important shortcuts & controls

SpacebarPlay / stop
J / K / LShuttle back / stop / forward (repeat to speed up)
S / MSolo / Mute a track
Cmd/Ctrl + SSave project

Common mistakes

  • Treating red meters as 'good and loud' — red means clipping.
  • Riding the track fader to fix one loud clip instead of using clip gain.
  • Exporting/re-importing audio instead of just switching to the Fairlight page.
  • Ignoring headroom and mixing everything near 0 dBFS.

Practical exercise

Open a finished interview edit, switch to Fairlight, and identify the Index, Mixer, timeline and transport. Solo each track in turn, watch the meters, and bring any clipping clip down with clip gain until peaks sit comfortably below 0 dBFS.

Exam preparation notes

  • Know the role of each panel: Index, Mixer, timeline, transport.
  • dBFS vs LUFS; 0 dBFS = clipping ceiling.
  • Clip gain vs track fader; mono/stereo/5.1/adaptive formats.
  • J-K-L and spacebar transport behaviour.

Module 02

Editing Dialogue

Chapter overview

Dialogue is the spine of talking-head, interview and corporate work. This module covers organising production sound, cutting cleanly, smoothing edits, and keeping picture and audio in sync — the day-to-day craft that makes speech sound effortless.

Detailed lesson notes

Organise first

  • Put each character / microphone on its own track so you can choose the best mic, balance levels and process each voice independently.
  • Keep and reuse room tone (ambient 'silence' recorded on location) — it fills gaps so cuts don't drop to dead digital silence.

Cutting & smoothing

  • Split clips with the razor / Cmd-Ctrl+B at the playhead.
  • Add small fades to clip edges and crossfades between overlapping takes — they remove the clicks caused by cutting on a non-zero waveform point.
  • Edit Selection (A) moves whole clips; Range Selection (R) selects a span of time across tracks regardless of clip boundaries.

Levelling & sync

  • Use clip gain / volume keyframes to lift quiet words and tame loud ones before compression.
  • Use snapping plus nudge keys for frame-accurate placement and lip-sync.
  • Link/group related clips so trims keep them in sync; disable (don't delete) a clip to silence it non-destructively.

Comping the best performance

  • A/B takes by soloing each in place.
  • Splice the clearest word or phrase from another take and match its level/EQ so the join is invisible.
  • Keep natural breaths (or gently reduce them) — stripping every breath makes speech robotic.

Key takeaways

  • Separate characters/mics onto their own tracks.
  • Fades and crossfades kill edit-point clicks.
  • Room tone fills gaps so cuts are inaudible.
  • Level with clip gain before EQ and compression.

Important shortcuts & controls

AEdit Selection mode (move whole clips)
RRange Selection mode (select a time span)
Cmd/Ctrl + BSplit clip at playhead
MAdd marker
NToggle snapping (commonly)

Common mistakes

  • Cutting without fades, leaving clicks at every edit.
  • Deleting room tone, then fighting dead-silent gaps.
  • Stripping every breath until speech sounds unnatural.
  • Compressing before levelling, so the compressor chases a moving target.

Practical exercise

Take a two-minute interview. Separate the speaker onto a clean track, remove an 'umm' and a chair creak, patch the gaps with room tone, add edge fades, and even out two quiet words with clip gain. The cut should sound continuous and natural.

Exam preparation notes

  • A vs R selection modes; razor/split.
  • Why fades/crossfades remove clicks.
  • Role of room tone in gap-filling.
  • Order: edit → clean → level → process.

Module 03

Sound Design & Effects Editing

Chapter overview

Sound design adds impact, realism and emotion. In Fairlight that means a disciplined spotting pass, smart use of the Sound Library, layering elements into custom effects, and building believable ambience — all routed so you can control whole layers at once.

Detailed lesson notes

Spot before you build

  • Do a spotting pass: place named markers (M) at every point that needs a sound. This is your build map.

The Sound Library

  • Search, preview/audition and drag in effects from the built-in library; you can mount your own folders so your whole collection is searchable in-app.

Layering & organisation

  • Keep hard FX (footsteps, slams), designed elements (whooshes, impacts, drones) and ambience on separate tracks.
  • Layer multiple elements into one richer effect (e.g. whoosh + low boom + transient).
  • Colour-code tracks by type (dialogue / Foley / ambience / SFX) so a dense session stays readable.

Control the layer with a bus

  • Route all SFX tracks to a single SFX sub-bus so one fader rides the entire effects layer.
  • Use clip gain for per-effect level tweaks without touching the rest.
  • Foley is performed everyday sound (footsteps, cloth, props) recorded to picture for realism.

Key takeaways

  • Spot with named markers first; design second.
  • Audition in the Sound Library, commit only the keeper.
  • Layer elements for custom, impactful sounds.
  • Bus a layer to one fader for instant control.

Important shortcuts & controls

MAdd marker (build your spotting list)
Double-click markerName/note the cue
Drag from Sound LibraryPlace an effect on a track

Common mistakes

  • Dropping effects randomly instead of spotting first.
  • Mixing SFX onto the dialogue track, losing independent control.
  • Relying on one thin library file instead of layering for weight.
  • Audible loop seams in short ambience beds (fix with crossfades).

Practical exercise

Spot a 30-second advert with named markers. Build one 'whoosh-to-impact' transition by layering three elements, add an ambience bed under the scene, route all SFX to one sub-bus, and balance the whole layer with a single fader.

Exam preparation notes

  • Spotting list via markers; Sound Library audition/mount.
  • Track separation: hard FX / design / ambience.
  • Layering concept; SFX sub-bus for group control.
  • Foley vs ADR distinction.

Module 04

Recording Voiceover & ADR

Chapter overview

Sometimes you must capture new audio: a narration to picture, or replacement dialogue when production sound is unusable. This module covers routing inputs, arming tracks, setting safe levels, the ADR panel, and blending new recordings into the scene.

Detailed lesson notes

Setting up to record

  • Use Patch Input/Output to route your mic/source input to a track and route tracks to outputs.
  • Record-enable (arm) only the target track — stray arms risk recording onto the wrong track.
  • Set a healthy level with headroom: peaks well below 0 dBFS. Clipped recordings can't be fixed.
  • Monitor on headphones when recording live to picture so speaker output doesn't bleed into the mic.

ADR (Automated Dialogue Replacement)

  • ADR re-records a line in sync to replace ruined production audio.
  • The ADR panel manages a cue list, take recording, and count-in beeps/streamers plus a guide track so the performer lands exactly on time.
  • Loop/punch recording lets you re-take a section without rebuilding the session.

Blending the new audio

  • Record multiple takes, then audition and comp the best performance.
  • Match level, EQ and the scene's space (room tone/reverb) so the new line sits believably — a pristine line stands out against a noisy scene.

Key takeaways

  • Patch input → arm the target track → set safe level → record.
  • Headphones prevent mic bleed when recording to picture.
  • ADR uses guide track + count-in cues for tight timing.
  • Blend new recordings by matching level, EQ and ambience.

Important shortcuts & controls

Arm/Record-enable buttonMarks the track that will capture audio
Patch Input/OutputRoute inputs to tracks, tracks to outputs
ADR panelCue list, guide track, count-in, take recording

Common mistakes

  • Recording with several tracks armed and overwriting the wrong one.
  • Monitoring on speakers and capturing bleed/feedback.
  • Peaking at 0 dBFS with no headroom.
  • Leaving ADR too 'clean' so it doesn't match the scene.

Practical exercise

Add a narration track, patch your mic, arm only that track, set levels for safe headroom on headphones, and record three takes of a line. Comp the best, then drop matching room tone under it so it blends with the scene.

Exam preparation notes

  • Patch Input/Output and arming workflow.
  • Why headphones for live-to-picture recording.
  • ADR = Automated Dialogue Replacement; guide track + cues.
  • Blending: level/EQ/ambience matching.

Module 05

Mixing

Chapter overview

Mixing balances every element into a clear, controlled whole. This module covers panning, EQ, dynamics (compressor, limiter, gate), buses and sends, and automation — with dialogue intelligibility as the constant priority.

Detailed lesson notes

Balance & space

  • The fader sets a track's overall level — your primary balancing tool.
  • Pan places sound across the stereo field. Keep lead dialogue centred; widen music/ambience to make space for the voice.

Tone & dynamics

  • EQ boosts/cuts frequency bands. A high-pass (low-cut) on dialogue clears rumble below the voice.
  • A compressor reduces dynamic range (evens loud/soft); threshold sets where it acts, ratio how hard.
  • A limiter is a brick-wall ceiling that stops peaks exceeding a set level. A noise gate silences signal below a threshold (cleaning gaps).
  • Track EQ shapes a whole track; Clip EQ fixes one clip; bus EQ shapes a group.

Routing & automation

  • A bus sums tracks. Main 1 is the master output. An Aux send feeds several tracks to one shared effect (e.g. reverb).
  • Automation records fader/pan/plugin moves that replay over time. Touch writes only while you hold the control; Latch keeps the last value; Write overwrites continuously.
  • Duck music under speech with level automation or side-chain ducking so dialogue always leads.
  • Monitor/Control-Room level changes only what you hear — never the actual mix.

Key takeaways

  • Dialogue leads: keep it centred and clear; duck the bed under it.
  • High-pass dialogue to clear rumble; compress to even dynamics.
  • Use sub-buses for cohesive group processing; aux sends for shared reverb.
  • Monitor level ≠ mix level.

Important shortcuts & controls

FaderTrack output level
S / MSolo / Mute while balancing
Automation mode (Touch/Latch/Write)How moves are recorded

Common mistakes

  • Letting dialogue get buried so it vanishes at low volume.
  • Compressing noisy audio hard, making it 'pump'.
  • Putting reverb on every clip instead of one aux send.
  • Mixing to a too-quiet monitor and over-pushing levels.

Practical exercise

Mix a talking-head with music: high-pass and gently compress the dialogue on a dialogue sub-bus, centre the voice, widen the music, and automate the music to duck under the presenter. Confirm the voice stays clear at low volume on a phone.

Exam preparation notes

  • Compressor threshold/ratio; limiter vs compressor; gate.
  • High-pass purpose on dialogue.
  • Bus vs Aux send; Main 1 as master.
  • Automation modes; monitor level vs mix level.

Module 06

Repairing & Restoring Audio

Chapter overview

Location work means rooms you can't treat: hum, hiss, clicks and reverberant spaces. Fairlight FX is the bundled plugin set for fixing them. The golden rule: diagnose first, repair before you mix, and use the least processing that works.

Detailed lesson notes

Know your tools (Fairlight FX)

  • De-Hummer: removes mains hum (50/60 Hz) and harmonics from location power/lighting.
  • De-Esser: tames harsh sibilance ('s', 'sh') without dulling the whole voice.
  • Noise Reduction: learn a profile from a voice-free moment, then subtract that signature. Use conservatively — too much makes voices watery/metallic.
  • De-Click / De-Crackle: remove short transient pops and crackle.
  • Voice Isolation (recent versions): AI separation that pulls clean dialogue out of noisy/reverberant rooms.

Method

  • Diagnose first: listen and identify each specific defect (hum, hiss, click, reverb) before choosing a tool. A frequency analyser/spectrogram helps you see tones you might miss.
  • Match repair scope to the problem: a one-off click gets a clip/sample-level fix; a consistent shoot-day defect gets a track-level chain or saved preset.
  • Repair before you mix: clean source makes EQ/compression/levels behave predictably; heavy compression on noisy audio pumps the noise.

Finishing

  • After heavy de-noising, lay a low bed of matching room tone so cleaned gaps feel natural, and restore lost presence with a gentle EQ lift.
  • A/B bypass every repair and check on multiple systems — confirm a real improvement, not just a change.
  • Clipping at the source is largely unrecoverable — mitigate, consider ADR, and capture clean levels next time.

Key takeaways

  • Diagnose, then pick the matching tool.
  • Noise Reduction needs a clean noise profile; less is more.
  • Repair before mixing.
  • Clipping is destructive — prevention beats repair.

Important shortcuts & controls

Zoom to sample levelFind and remove individual clicks
Insert on clip vs trackFix one clip vs process the whole track
Bypass (A/B)Verify the repair genuinely helps

Common mistakes

  • Over-applying noise reduction, leaving watery/metallic artefacts.
  • Compressing before repairing, so the noise pumps.
  • Blanket Main-bus repair that harms clean elements.
  • Trusting the waveform instead of listening on multiple systems.

Practical exercise

Take an untreated-room interview with AC hiss and a single cable click. Capture a noise profile from a clean moment and reduce the hiss conservatively, de-click the single pop at sample level, lay subtle room tone under the cleaned gaps, then A/B bypass to confirm improvement.

Exam preparation notes

  • De-Hummer / De-Esser / Noise Reduction / De-Click roles.
  • Noise profile learning; artefacts from over-processing.
  • Repair-before-mix order; scope matching.
  • Clipping is unrecoverable; Voice Isolation for rooms.

Module 07

Delivering & Mastering

Chapter overview

Delivery makes your mix meet the spec of wherever it plays. This module covers loudness (LUFS), true peak, normalisation, the loudness meter, stems, and the Deliver page audio settings — so the same content lands correctly on broadcast, web and social.

Detailed lesson notes

Loudness & true peak

  • Perceived loudness is measured in LUFS. Targets: broadcast ≈ -23 LUFS (EBU R128) / -24 LKFS (US ATSC A/85); online/social ≈ -14 LUFS; spoken-word/podcast ≈ -16 LUFS.
  • Cap True Peak around -1 dBTP so lossy encoding doesn't push peaks into clipping.
  • The Fairlight loudness meter shows Momentary, Short-term and Integrated (whole-programme average) — Integrated is what specs reference.

Hitting the target

  • Audio Normalization can target a peak level or a loudness standard (e.g. R128) to reach a target automatically.
  • Watch the Main bus meter before export: no clipping, correct loudness, safe true peak.
  • A clean master near -14 LUFS / -1 dBTP travels well across most social platforms; don't mix far hotter just to be turned down.

Stems & export

  • Set codec, sample rate and bit depth on the Deliver page → render settings → Audio tab. Professional masters are 48 kHz / 24-bit.
  • Deliver D/M/E stems (separate dialogue, music, effects buses) so clients can re-balance, re-version or dub. Keep dialogue separate + M&E for translation.
  • Confirm every sub-bus is routed to the Main and do a full-listen QC before sending.

Key takeaways

  • LUFS targets differ by platform; integrated loudness is the spec number.
  • True peak ≈ -1 dBTP protects against encoding clipping.
  • Stems (D/M/E) enable re-balancing and translation.
  • Deliver 48 kHz / 24-bit; QC by listening before sending.

Important shortcuts & controls

Deliver page → Audio tabCodec, sample rate, bit depth
Loudness meterIntegrated / Short-term / Momentary LUFS
Audio NormalizationHit a peak or loudness target automatically

Common mistakes

  • Mastering to 0 dBFS / ignoring loudness specs.
  • Forgetting to route a sub-bus to the Main (missing layer on export).
  • Delivering a flat mix when the client needs stems for translation.
  • Exporting without a full-listen QC.

Practical exercise

Master a finished talking-head to ≈ -14 LUFS with true peak ≤ -1 dBTP, verify on the loudness meter, route D/M/E to separate buses, and export both the full mix and the stems at 48 kHz / 24-bit. QC the file end-to-end before 'delivery'.

Exam preparation notes

  • LUFS targets: -23/-24 broadcast, -14 online, -16 podcast.
  • True peak ≈ -1 dBTP; Integrated loudness meaning.
  • Audio Normalization modes; 48 kHz/24-bit masters.
  • Stems & M&E for re-versioning/translation; route to Main.
